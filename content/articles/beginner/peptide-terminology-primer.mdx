Peptide conversations are thick with jargon. Some of it is borrowed from pharmacology, some from endocrinology, some from injection practice, and some is community shorthand. This article is a reference sheet — skim it once, come back when you hit something confusing.

## Units and doses

**mcg (microgram)** — one thousandth of a milligram. Most peptide doses are measured in mcg. 500mcg = 0.5mg.

**mg (milligram)** — one thousandth of a gram. Vial sizes are usually in mg (2mg, 5mg, 10mg, etc.).

**IU (International Unit)** — used for growth hormone (HGH) and insulin, not most peptides. For HGH, 1mg ≈ 3 IU.

**Units (on an insulin syringe)** — the 1–100 markings on a U-100 insulin syringe. 100 units = 1mL. So 10 units = 0.1mL, 50 units = 0.5mL.

**Concentration (mg/mL)** — how much peptide is dissolved in each milliliter of solution after reconstitution. This is the number you need to calculate doses. See [Reconstitution 101](https://pepatlas.com/articles/reconstitution-101).

## Injection routes

**Sub-Q / SC (subcutaneous)** — injected into the fatty layer just under the skin. Most peptides. Uses short, thin needles. Belly fat is typical.

**IM (intramuscular)** — injected into muscle. Longer, thicker needles. Used for some peptides (HCG, some GH peptides), most hormonal injections, and vaccines.

**IV (intravenous)** — directly into a vein. Rarely used for research peptides. Higher-risk route.

**IN (intranasal)** — nasal spray. Semax and Selank are the common examples.

**Oral** — swallowed. Works for some peptides (KPV, certain BPC-157 protocols, semaglutide in oral form as Rybelsus).

**Topical / transdermal** — applied to skin. GHK-Cu in creams is the main one.

## Receptor and signaling terms

**Receptor** — a protein on (or inside) a cell that a peptide binds to, triggering a response.

**Agonist** — a compound that activates a receptor. Most therapeutic peptides are agonists.

**Antagonist** — a compound that blocks a receptor without activating it.

**Partial agonist** — activates the receptor less fully than a full agonist would.

**Desensitization / tachyphylaxis** — when a receptor becomes less responsive over time with continued stimulation. Why some peptides need cycling.

**Half-life** — how long it takes for half of a dose to be cleared from the body. A 30-minute half-life means frequent dosing; a 7-day half-life (like semaglutide) means weekly dosing.

## Hormone-axis shorthand

**GHRH (Growth Hormone Releasing Hormone)** — the natural hormone that tells your pituitary to release growth hormone. Sermorelin, CJC-1295, and Tesamorelin are GHRH analogs.

**GHRP (Growth Hormone Releasing Peptide)** — a different class that stimulates GH release through the ghrelin receptor. Ipamorelin, GHRP-2, GHRP-6, and Hexarelin are GHRPs.

**GHS (Growth Hormone Secretagogue)** — umbrella term for anything that makes you release more GH. Includes both GHRHs and GHRPs.

**HPTA (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Testicular Axis)** — the feedback loop controlling testosterone production. Most peptides don't suppress it. Anabolic steroids and SARMs do.

**GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1)** — a gut hormone influencing appetite and blood sugar. Semaglutide, liraglutide, and dulaglutide are GLP-1 agonists. Tirzepatide is a dual GLP-1 / GIP agonist.

**GIP (Glucose-Dependent Insulinotropic Polypeptide)** — another gut hormone. Tirzepatide and retatrutide target it alongside GLP-1.

## Product and quality terms

**COA (Certificate of Analysis)** — third-party lab test of a peptide batch. See [How to Read a COA](https://pepatlas.com/articles/how-to-read-a-coa).

**Lyophilized** — freeze-dried. Almost all research peptides ship as lyophilized white powder in small glass vials.

**Reconstitution** — mixing lyophilized powder with bacteriostatic water to make an injectable solution.

**Bac water (bacteriostatic water)** — sterile water with 0.9% benzyl alcohol added to prevent microbial growth. The standard solvent for reconstituting peptides.

**HPLC** — high-performance liquid chromatography. The main method for measuring peptide purity.

**Mass spec / LC-MS** — mass spectrometry. Confirms the peptide is actually what the label says.

**RUO (Research Use Only)** — the legal framework most research peptide vendors operate under. See [Research Use vs Personal Use](https://pepatlas.com/articles/research-use-vs-personal-use).

## Biological terms that come up

**Angiogenesis** — formation of new blood vessels. BPC-157 and TB-500 promote this; it's part of how they support healing.

**Anti-inflammatory** — reduces inflammation. A very common description of peptide effects, but the mechanism varies a lot by compound.

**Bioavailability** — the fraction of a dose that actually reaches circulation. Injected peptides: close to 100%. Oral peptides: often <5% (which is why most are injected).

**Endogenous** — produced by your body.

**Exogenous** — introduced from outside (the peptides you inject).

**Pharmacokinetics (PK)** — how a drug moves through the body: absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion. Governs dosing frequency.

**Pharmacodynamics (PD)** — what the drug does once it's in the body. Governs the effect you're looking for.

## Community shorthand

**Stack** — a combination of peptides run together. "Healing stack" = BPC-157 + TB-500, typically.

**Cycle / on-cycle / off-cycle** — time periods running a protocol vs. taking a break. Common on GH peptides.

**Pin** — slang for inject. "Pinning daily" = injecting daily.

**PIP (Post-Injection Pain)** — soreness or irritation after an injection. Mild with peptides; significant with some oil-based hormonal injections.

**Blast / cruise** — terms borrowed from steroid culture for high-dose and maintenance-dose periods. Occasionally appears in GH peptide discussion.

**Grey-market / gray-market** — the research chemical market.

**Lab** / **source** — community shorthand for vendor.
