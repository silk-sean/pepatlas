A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is the third-party test report for a specific batch of peptide. At minimum it should tell you what's in the vial, how pure it is, and confirm the molecule is what the label says. A real COA comes from an independent lab and references a specific batch or lot number on the product.

Vendors hand-wave this. "99% purity!" on a website is marketing. A COA is data. Learn to read one and you're no longer trusting a tagline.

## What you need to see on a COA

A legitimate COA includes:

- [ ] Lab name and accreditation (ISO 17025 or equivalent is the gold standard)
- [ ] Date of testing
- [ ] Compound name and sequence
- [ ] Batch or lot number (must match the number on your vial)
- [ ] HPLC (purity) result with chromatogram attached
- [ ] Mass spectrometry result with spectrum attached
- [ ] Signature or electronic stamp from the analyst

Missing the chromatogram or spectrum means missing the actual data. A single-page summary with "99%" typed on it is not a COA.

## HPLC — what it proves

HPLC (high-performance liquid chromatography) separates the compounds in a sample. The chromatogram is a series of peaks; each peak is a distinct substance.

For a peptide sample:

- **One large peak** = the target peptide
- **Small peaks** around it = impurities, degradation fragments, deletion sequences
- **Purity %** = area of the target peak divided by total area of all peaks

**What's good:**
- 98%+ is standard for modern research-grade peptides
- 99%+ is common for premium vendors
- <95% should give you pause; <90% is a red flag unless it's a known complex compound

**What to look at on the chromatogram itself:**
- A single dominant, symmetrical peak — good
- A shoulder or second peak right next to the main peak — that's often a deletion sequence or oxidation product, worth asking about
- A forest of small peaks along the baseline — degraded or poorly synthesized

## Mass spec — what it proves

Mass spectrometry measures the molecular weight of the compound. It confirms **identity** — that what's in the vial is actually the peptide claimed.

Every peptide has a known theoretical molecular weight. A mass spec result should match that weight within a narrow tolerance (typically ±1 Da for small peptides).

**Example:** BPC-157 theoretical MW is 1419.5 Da. A legitimate COA for BPC-157 should show a mass peak at ~1419.5 (or sometimes a proton-adduct peak at ~1420.5, depending on the method).

If the mass on the COA doesn't match the theoretical weight of the compound, you either have the wrong molecule or a fabricated report.

## Quick COA checklist

| Check | Legit COA | Red flag |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| Lab name | Named, accredited, Google-able | Missing or "internal QC" |
| Lot number | Matches vial | Missing or generic |
| HPLC chromatogram | Full chart included | Just a "%" number |
| Mass spec spectrum | Full spectrum included | Just a "MW matches" line |
| Test date | Recent, within batch life | Old, or blank |
| Signed by analyst | Yes | No |
| PDF metadata | Created near test date | Created days before you asked about it |

## What a COA doesn't tell you

- **Sterility.** HPLC and mass spec don't test for bacteria or endotoxin. A peptide can be 99% pure and still contaminated.
- **Shelf life remaining.** A COA is a snapshot from when the batch was made. If it's two years old, storage matters more than the original number.
- **What's in *your* vial specifically.** The COA is for the lot. Poor handling between lab and you can degrade the vial you hold.

## Common mistakes

- **Trusting an image of a COA without checking the lab.** Google the lab name. If they don't exist or don't respond to contact requests, the COA might be fabricated.
- **Reading only the purity %.** A 99% mark without a chromatogram is marketing, not data.
- **Ignoring lot mismatch.** If your vial says "Lot 2024-117" and the COA says "Lot 2023-042," that COA doesn't apply to your product.
- **Assuming a COA from one batch covers everything the vendor ships.** Reputable vendors issue a COA per batch. Using a single decade-old COA across all their compounds is lazy at best, misleading at worst.
- **Not saving COAs.** Screenshot or PDF the COA for every order. If something goes wrong, you'll want the documentation.
